Abstract

The advent of large-scale diffusion models has greatly enhanced the quality of generated videos; however, their application in real-time streaming remains limited. While autoregressive models offer a natural framework for sequential frame gener- ation, they typically demand substantial computational resources to achieve high visual fidelity. Although diffusion distillation techniques can compress such models into efficient few-step variants, current video distillation methods largely adopt image-specific approaches that overlook critical temporal dependencies. Conse- quently, these techniques often excel in image generation but underperform in video synthesis, leading to issues such as reduced motion coherence, error accumulation over long sequences, and a persistent trade-off between latency and quality. We identify that these challenges are primarily exacerbated by two factors: insufficient use of temporal context during step reduction and the implicit prediction of the next noise level in next-chunk prediction (exposure bias). To address these issues, we propose a novel framework—diagonal distillation—that is orthogonal to existing approaches. This method systematically leverages temporal information across both video chunks and denoising steps. Specifically, we introduce an asymmetric generation strategy: “more steps early, fewer steps later.” This allows later chunks to efficiently inherit rich appearance information from the initial, more thoroughly processed chunks, while also utilizing partially denoised chunks as conditional inputs for subsequent generation. By realigning the implicit prediction of the next noise level during chunk generation with actual inference conditions, our approach mitigates error propagation and alleviates oversaturation in long-range sequences. Additionally, we integrate implicit optical flow modeling to preserve motion quality under strict step constraints. Our experiments demonstrate that the proposed framework establishes a new state-of-the-art(SOTA) in efficient video generation and reduces denoising computational cost by 68.5% during denoising, while achieving comparable or even superior video quality relative to the previous SOTA method.
